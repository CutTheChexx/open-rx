# Flux: The Content Treatment System

Flux is your prescription for content paralysis. It's a complete operating system for creators, solopreneurs, agencies, and businesses that need to write at scale without losing their voice.

This skill covers everything: from finding your brand voice to publishing ghostwritten books, from batching social content to building content pillars that actually drive revenue. It's not about "AI assistance." It's about writing systems that work.

---

## 1. Brand Voice Development

**The Problem:** Most creators sound like everyone else. You're competing against 100,000 identical LinkedIn posts and "thought leadership" articles written by the same AI model.

### Finding Your Voice

Your voice is not invented—it's excavated.

1. **Audit yourself**: Pull 10-20 pieces you've written. What words repeat? What punctuation patterns? What metaphors? What's your natural cadence?
2. **Identify your assumptions**: What do you assume your audience already knows? Where do you get impatient with slow explanations?
3. **Document your opinions**: Not vague takes. Specific, defensible positions on your industry. These become your voice anchors.
4. **Steal from everywhere**: Copy the voice DNA of writers you admire. Not their words—their patterns, speed, rhythm, directness.

### Voice Attributes Framework

Every voice has dimensions. Map yours:

- **Speed**: Fast (choppy, punchy) ↔ Slow (elaborate, meditative)
- **Formality**: Casual (curse words OK) ↔ Professional (measured, corporate)
- **Optimism**: Cynical (problems first) ↔ Idealistic (solutions first)
- **Jargon**: Dense (insider terms) ↔ Plain (explaining everything)
- **Humor**: Dry ↔ Explicit ↔ None
- **Authority**: Expert (you know) ↔ Peer (learning together)

Write these down. When you're tired and writing without thinking, these attributes prevent drift.

### Tone Spectrum

Your voice is static. Your tone moves. Same person, different contexts.

**Example for a SaaS founder:**
- Customer onboarding: Encouraging, simple, no jargon
- Industry article: Authoritative, data-backed, insider language OK
- Twitter: Punchy, opinionated, direct
- Internal updates: Vulnerable, honest about challenges

Create a tone spectrum document. Each channel/context gets a description, not rules.

### Voice Guidelines Template


## 2. Blog Writing System

**Blogs are your owned media.** Every social post is a rent payment. Every email list is borrowed. Blogs are real estate.

### Post Structures

Different goals need different structures.

**How-To Posts** (SEO gold, solves immediate problems)
1. Hook: Problem statement + why it matters
2. Prerequisites: What they need before starting
3. Step-by-step instructions: Numbered, clear, no assumptions
4. Why each step matters: The reasoning, not just the action
5. Common mistakes: Anticipate where people get stuck
6. Variations: "If X, do this instead"
7. Conclusion: Next skill or related post link

**Listicles** (Scannable, shareable, evergreen)
1. Headline: "7 [adjective] [nouns] That [verb]"
2. Intro: Why you should care about this list
3. Each item: Headline + 100-150 words of explanation
4. Tie the list together: How these items relate
5. Conclusion: One action they can take now
6. *Don't just list.* Explain why each item matters.

**Case Studies** (Credibility, specificity)
1. Hook: "We took [type of business] from $X to $Y"
2. The situation: Who were they? What was broken?
3. What we tried: The specific tactics (don't hide the details)
4. What worked: Why it worked, not just that it did
5. What failed: The experiments that didn't pan out
6. Results: Numbers, timelines, proof
7. Lessons: What applies to your reader, not just this client

**Opinion Posts** (Builds authority, sparks conversation)
1. Take: Your controversial/unpopular opinion (first sentence)
2. Why you believe it: Personal experience, data, observation
3. Counter-arguments: Steelman the other side
4. Your rebuttal: Why you're still right
5. Who this applies to: Explicitly say "this advice is for X, not Y"
6. Call to action: Disagree with you? Show me your data.

### SEO Writing Without Sacrificing Voice

- **Research intent first**: What are people actually searching for? Not what you want to write about.
- **Front-load the keyword**: In headline, first paragraph, and first H2. Then forget about it.
- **Write for humans**: Keyword stuffing kills voice. Weave it in naturally.
- **Use headers aggressively**: H2s, H3s. Search engines and readers both appreciate them.
- **Internal linking strategy**: Link to your previous posts on related topics. Build a web, not a collection of islands.
- **Length matters, but voice matters more**: 3,000 words of garbage loses to 1,200 words of insight.

### Hooks That Actually Work

Your opening 2-3 sentences determine if someone reads or bounces.

**Pattern-interrupt hooks:**
- "You're being lied to about [common belief]"
- "The opposite of [advice everyone gives] might be true"
- "I spent $50K learning this, so you don't have to"

**Curiosity hooks:**
- "This sounds backwards, but..."
- "Nobody talks about [obvious thing]"
- "I discovered this by accident"

**Problem hooks:**
- "If you're struggling with [specific problem], you're not alone"
- "This costs most people [money/time] per year"
- "The standard solution doesn't work. Here's why"

**Story hooks:**
- "Last year, I was [situation]. Then [turning point]. Now [result]."

*Test them.* See what your audience clicks.

### Conclusions That Drive Action

Your ending determines if they leave and forget or leave and do something.

**Good conclusion pattern:**
1. Restate the core insight (one sentence)
2. What success looks like: "If you implement this, in 30 days you'll see..."
3. Next step: Specific, small, doable today
4. If they want deeper: Link to related content or your service

Don't end with "Thanks for reading." End with what's next.

## 3. Email Marketing

**Email is the only channel you own.** Treat it like real estate, not a distribution list.

### Welcome Sequence (5-7 emails over 2 weeks)

**Email 1 (Immediate):** Deliver the promised value
- "Here's the [resource/ebook/discount] you signed up for"
- Brief context on what it is
- One clear next step

**Email 2 (Day 2):** Story of why you do this work
- Origin story: How did you get here? What problem were you solving?
- Make it specific, not generic
- One clear belief you have

**Email 3 (Day 4):** Common misconception you had
- "I used to think [wrong thing]"
- What changed your mind
- How your audience might be thinking the same way

**Email 4 (Day 7):** Case study or concrete example
- Real situation, real results
- Shows what's possible
- Positions your future offer

**Email 5 (Day 10):** Behind-the-scenes or vulnerability
- Shows you're human
- Builds trust
- Keeps the relationship warm

**Email 6 (Day 14):** Soft introduction to what you offer
- "Here's what I do and who it's for"
- Clear statement of who it's NOT for
- Link to learn more, no pressure

### Newsletters That People Actually Open

- **Consistent schedule**: Same day, same time every week
- **Lead with value**: Useful insight, link, or resource first
- **Write conversationally**: Short sentences. Fragments. Questions.
- **Personal note**: Include something about your week, not just content
- **Small CTAs**: "What are your thoughts?" builds relationship more than "Buy now"
- **Segmentation**: Different subscribers want different things

### Promotional Emails (That Don't Feel Slimy)

**Pattern that works:**
1. Acknowledge why people are skeptical of your pitch
2. Share why you built this specific thing
3. Show who it's for (and who it's explicitly not for)
4. Specific result or transformation
5. How to get started
6. Clear deadline if there is one (honest, not fake urgency)

**Frequency rule**: Don't promote more than 1 week per month without providing pure value the other weeks.

### Re-engagement Campaigns

When opens drop and unsubscribes rise:

1. **Audit email 1**: Did the welcome sequence break? That's your leak.
2. **Identify the shift**: When did engagement drop? What changed?
3. **Re-engagement email**: "Hey, your email has gone quiet. Here's what's new. Still want in?" Give them an out—make unsubscribe easy and guilt-free.
4. **Win-back campaign**: If someone hasn't opened in 60 days, one final email. "We miss you. Here's something genuinely good. See you soon?"

### Subject Line Formulas

These work:

- "I finally understand [thing everyone wonders about]"
- "[Name], I made something for you"
- "Can we be honest about [industry assumption]?"
- "This will save you [X hours/dollars] per week"
- "The [problem] nobody talks about"
- "I was wrong about [thing]"

**Rules:**
- Test your name: "Hello Richard" vs "Hey Richard" vs just no name at all
- No fake urgency: Don't use urgency unless it's real
- Specificity beats cleverness: "3 mistakes your SaaS marketing team makes" beats "This will blow your mind"

## 4. Social Media Content Strategy

**Social media is your broadcast channel, but each platform is a different language.**

### Platform-Specific Strategies

**X/Twitter** (Algorithm rewards: retweets, likes, replies)
- Short, punchy takes: 280 characters of insight
- Threads for depth: 5-7 connected tweets, numbered
- Respond to what's trending: Add your perspective to current conversations
- Ask questions: More engagement than statements
- Share data/images: Threads with screenshots outperform text-only
- Frequency: 1-5 posts per day depending on your strategy

**LinkedIn** (Algorithm rewards: comments on your post, profile visits, connection requests)
- Long-form posts: 300-500 words, formatted with breaks
- Story-driven: Narrative > tips list (opposite of Twitter)
- Lead with vulnerability: "I failed because..." generates more comments
- Target a specific person: "If you're a CMO of a B2B SaaS company..."
- Engagement bait works here (cynical but true): "Agree or disagree:" followed by take
- Frequency: 2-4 posts per week max (quality over volume)

**Instagram** (Algorithm rewards: saves, shares, DMs, time spent)
- Visual-first: The image matters more than caption
- Caption patterns: Hook (1-2 lines) → Story (the meat) → CTA (save this, share with friend)
- Reels > static posts (2024+): Short-form video is the feed
- Captions long enough to encourage "show more": ~150 words of depth
- Frequency: 3-5 posts per week (mix of posts, reels, stories)

**TikTok** (Algorithm rewards: watch time, re-watches, shares, DMs)
- Hook in first second: Most people decide in 0.5 seconds if they'll keep watching
- Trending sounds/music: Work with trends, don't ignore them
- Value or entertainment: Educational content wins if it's genuinely useful, not just tips
- Personality over perfection: TikTok rewards realness, not polish
- Frequency: 1-3 per day if you're serious (volume is part of the game)

### Content Calendars That Don't Suck

**Template structure by week:**
- **Monday**: What you learned this week (educational, vulnerable)
- **Wednesday**: Case study or concrete example (proof your advice works)
- **Friday**: Opinion or hot take (engagement, personality)
- **Bonus**: Curated content from others you respect (builds community)

**Monthly themes**: Choose one pillar topic per month. All four week's content relates back. Builds topical authority.

**Batching schedule**: Spend 2 hours every other Sunday writing and scheduling. Don't live-tweet through your week.

### Engagement Patterns

- **Response time**: Reply to comments within 6 hours. Early comments drive algorithm.
- **Ask questions**: Posts with questions get 2x engagement.
- **Controversial (but honest)**: Stakes drive engagement. Boring drives unsubscribes.
- **Tag people sparingly**: Tagged people see it, but algorithm doesn't reward tagging.
- **Hashtag strategy**: Use 5-10 on Instagram (targets specific intent), 2-3 on Twitter (looks awkward), 0-3 on LinkedIn (looks spammy)

## 5. Long-Form Content (Books, Guides, Whitepapers, Ebooks)

**Long-form is credibility. It's the ultimate sales page.**

### Outlining Strategy

Before you write a word:

1. **Define the reader promise**: "After reading this, you'll be able to..."
2. **Work backwards**: What do they need to know to achieve that promise?
3. **Break it into chapters**: Each chapter solves one problem or teaches one thing
4. **Chapter structure**: Each chapter should take 15-20 minutes to read
5. **Dependencies**: Which chapters must come before others? Map it out.

**Sample outline for a guide:**
```
Introduction: Why you need this
Chapter 1: The problem (validate that it matters)
Chapter 2: Why you haven't solved it yet (psychology/mindset)
Chapter 3: The framework (your solution)
Chapter 4: Implementation (how to actually do it)
Chapter 5: Case study (proof it works)
Chapter 6: Common obstacles (what could go wrong)
Conclusion: What's next
```

### Chapter Structure

Every chapter needs:

1. **Opening**: Why this chapter matters to your reader (not a summary)
2. **The core idea**: Your main teaching point
3. **Proof**: Data, example, or story supporting it
4. **Application**: "Here's how you do this"
5. **Why it works**: The reasoning, so they can adapt it
6. **Closing**: Link to next chapter or recap

**Length**: 2,500-4,000 words per chapter. Long enough to be meaty, short enough to finish in one sitting.

### Writing Cadence

- **Sprint model**: Dedicate 1-2 weeks to writing intensely. Not an hour here, hour there.
- **First draft first**: Write fast, edit slow. Don't edit while writing.
- **Accountability**: Tell people you're writing. Post progress. Sunken cost helps.
- **Deadline**: Publish on a date. Hard deadline beats "when it's done."

### Ebook-Specific Tactics

- **Lead magnet**: Ebook is the payoff for an email signup (100-150 pages max)
- **Gated properly**: Email required to download, not a link on your homepage
- **Designed well**: Poor design = high bounce. Good design = keeps people reading
- **Calls to action**: Minimal (1-2 per ebook). Let it breathe. The CTA is buying your course or service later.

### Whitepaper Tactics

- **Research-heavy**: Data, statistics, research backing claims
- **Professional design**: Whitepapers are more formal than ebooks
- **Gated**: B2B play. Email required.
- **Length**: 5-15 pages. Dense but not overwhelming.
- **Format**: Usually PDF with proper formatting

## 6. Ghostwriting Framework

**Writing someone else's voice while maintaining your own writing systems.**

### Voice Matching Process

1. **Collect source material**:
   - 10-20 pieces they've written (emails, blog posts, interviews)
   - Interviews with them (record, transcribe)
   - Internal documents with their phrasing

2. **Build a voice map**:
   - What words do they use repeatedly?
   - Sentence structure: Short and punchy or long and elaborate?
   - Metaphors: What do they compare things to?
   - Tics: Do they have linguistic habits?

3. **Write a voice guide** (for yourself):
   ```
   [Client Name] Voice
   - Speed: [slow/fast]
   - Key phrases they use: [list 20]
   - Humor style: [describe]
   - What they care about: [list 3-5 core values/obsessions]
   - What they avoid: [things they never say]
   - Their best thinking: [distill their unique perspective]
   ```

4. **Test it**: Write 3 short paragraphs. Show them. Adjust.

### Interview-to-Content Pipeline

1. **Pre-interview prep**:
   - Send them the outline/topic
   - Ask them to jot down 3 stories or examples
   - 30-minute call, not 90-minute

2. **During interview**:
   - Record (with permission)
   - Ask follow-up questions: "Why does that matter? Tell me more about that."
   - Listen for moments of passion—those become the writing
   - Don't write during the call, listen

3. **Post-interview**:
   - Transcribe
   - Pull out 3-4 best quotes
   - Extract the core teaching point
   - Map it to the outline

4. **Writing**:
   - Write in their voice using their phrasing
   - Weave in their stories naturally
   - Use their quotes for emphasis, not crutches
   - Send draft for approval: they should recognize themselves

### Maintaining Someone Else's Voice

- **Use their metaphors**: Not your metaphors. Theirs.
- **Match their pace**: If they speak in short sentences, write in short sentences.
- **Preserve their opinions**: Don't soften their takes.
- **Transparent authorship**: Usually "As told to [your name]" or "Ghostwritten by [your name]"
- **Rounds of feedback**: 2-3 revision rounds is normal. They're checking your voice match.

## 7. Content Repurposing: One Piece → Many Formats

**Write once, ship everywhere.**

### The Repurposing Framework

Start with your core asset (usually a blog post or long-form piece):

**Blog Post → Email Series (5-7 emails)**
- Break the post into digestible chunks
- One key insight per email
- Spread over 2 weeks
- Each email can stand alone

**Blog Post → Twitter Thread**
- Find the core insight
- Break into 5-7 connected tweets
- Add specific examples or stats
- End with a question or resource link

**Blog Post → LinkedIn Post**
- Write 300-word version focusing on the story
- Lead with vulnerability or opinion
- CTA: "Read the full guide" linking to post

**Blog Post → Visual Graphics (Instagram/Pinterest)**
- Extract key statistics or quotes
- 2-3 images maximum
- Caption that teases the deeper content
- Link to full post

**Blog Post → Video Script**
- Write a 5-10 minute video (1,500-2,000 words spoken)
- Start with hook (what they'll learn)
- Structure: Same as blog, but with B-roll
- End with CTA (link in description)

**Blog Post → Podcast Episode Outline**
- Interview yourself or guest on the topic
- 20-30 minute episode
- Chapters match blog sections
- Transcript becomes another content piece

**Blog Post → Email Course/Sequence**
- Break into 5-7 emails
- Each email teaches one thing
- Delivered daily or every other day
- Creates cohesive learning experience

### The Repurposing Timeline

**Week 1**: Publish blog post
**Week 2**: Email series launches
**Week 3**: Twitter thread
**Week 4**: Video goes live
**Week 5**: Podcast episode drops
**Week 6**: Graphic series on Instagram

Spread the repurposing over 5-6 weeks so each format feels fresh.

## 8. Writing Productivity: Batching, Templates, Swipe Files

**Treat writing like a system, not inspiration.**

### Batch Writing (How to Write 20 Blogs in a Week)

1. **Choose your topic**: One pillar topic for the week
2. **Outline day**: Spend 4 hours creating outlines for all 20 posts
3. **Structure day**: Fill in each outline with main points (not full prose)
4. **Writing day 1**: Write posts 1-5 (one outline → full blog)
5. **Writing day 2**: Write posts 6-10
6. **Writing day 3**: Write posts 11-15
7. **Writing day 4**: Write posts 16-20
8. **Editing day**: Read through all 20, light edits, schedule

**Why it works**: Your brain stays in "blog writing mode." Context switching kills productivity.

### Writing Templates (The Template Effect)

Templates aren't crutches. They're guardrails that let you write faster.

**Email template** (120 seconds to fill in):
```
Subject: [Hook about their pain point]

Hi [Name],

[2-3 sentence story about why you're writing]

Here's what I learned:
- [Insight 1]
- [Insight 2]
- [Insight 3]

The big takeaway: [One sentence core insight]

Next step: [One action they can take]

[Your name]
```

**Blog post template** (fill in the blanks, write the sections):
```
# [Headline with keyword]

[Hook paragraph: 2-3 sentences, make me care]

## Why This Matters

[1 paragraph on the problem/opportunity]

## The Solution

[3-4 paragraphs, one idea per section]

## How to Implement This

[Step-by-step, numbered]

## Common Mistakes

[2-3 mistakes people make, how to avoid them]

## Case Study

[Real example of this working]

## Conclusion

[Recap + next step]
```

Fill in the blanks. Write the sections. Done in 3 hours instead of 5.

### Swipe Files (The Competitive Advantage)

A swipe file is a collection of good writing—copy, headlines, structures, templates—that you reference when stuck.

**What to collect:**
- Subject lines that made you open an email
- Headline structures that caught your attention
- Email sequences that worked
- Blog post openings that hooked you
- Social media captions that drove engagement
- Sales pages with good copy

**Organization:**
- Google Doc or Notion database
- Organize by type (subject line, headline, email, etc.)
- Add context: "Why did this work?"
- Don't copy directly—use as inspiration only

**Review schedule**: Spend 15 minutes every Sunday adding to your swipe file. Before writing anything important, reference it.

### Overcoming Writer's Block

**It's not inspiration, it's systems failure.**

- **Procrastination mask**: You're not blocked, you're avoiding the hard thinking. Do the outline first.
- **Perfectionism mask**: You're trying to write perfectly on draft 1. Give yourself permission to write badly.
- **Comparison mask**: You're reading other writers and feeling small. Close the browser, write yours.
- **Wrong project mask**: You're writing the wrong post. Switch to something you care about.

**Emergency tactic**: Write the worst version possible in 30 minutes. Timer on. No editing. Just vomit it out. Editing that is easier than starting.

## 9. Content Strategy: Pillars, Clusters, Editorial Calendar

**Strategy separates full-time creators from hobbyists.**

### Defining Your Content Pillars

Content pillars are the 3-5 main topics your brand sits on. Everything you create relates back.

**Example: SaaS Growth Expert**
- Pillar 1: Product-led growth
- Pillar 2: Sales onboarding
- Pillar 3: Retention & expansion
- Pillar 4: Pricing & packaging
- Pillar 5: Case studies & benchmarks

Each blog post, email, and social post connects to one pillar.

**Why it matters**: Search engines recognize you as an expert on these topics. Readers know what to expect.

### Topic Clusters (Topical Authority)

Under each pillar, create clusters of related content.

**Example cluster under "Product-Led Growth":**
- The 5 principles of PLG (core post)
  - Free trials and freemium (related post)
  - Product onboarding (related post)
  - Viral loops & referral (related post)
  - Self-serve monetization (related post)

Each cluster has a "hub" (the main post) with internal links to the spokes.

**SEO benefit**: Google sees you've comprehensively covered a topic. Ranks you higher.

### Editorial Calendar (3 Months Rolling)

Map content month by month:

**Month 1:**
- Week 1: New hub post (Pillar 1)
- Week 2: Spoke post + email series + Twitter thread
- Week 3: New hub post (Pillar 2)
- Week 4: Spoke post + case study + LinkedIn

**Month 2:**
- Rotate pillars, repeat structure

**Month 3:**
- Plan ahead while executing current month

This rhythm ensures consistency without burnout.

### Content-Market Fit Assessment

Your content is working if:

1. **Views**: Increasing 10-20% month-over-month
2. **Engagement**: Comments and shares on every post
3. **Conversions**: People signing up for your email list or product
4. **Inbound**: Prospects mention your content in sales calls
5. **Authority**: Competitors are quoting your posts

If you're not seeing these signals, audit:
- Are you writing about what your market cares about, or what you think they should care about?
- Is your content findable? (SEO, distribution)
- Are you asking for action? (CTAs matter)

## 10. Anti-Patterns: What Kills Content

### AI Slop Writing (The Death of Distinction)

**The problem**: AI-generated writing is recognizable now. It's middle-of-the-road, voiceless, and unmemorable.

**Tells of AI slop:**
- Every paragraph is the same length
- Word choice is corporate and safe ("leverage," "utilize," "synergy")
- No personality. No conviction.
- Examples are generic instead of specific
- No contradictions or messy realness

**How to avoid it:**
- Never publish AI writing without heavy voice overlay
- Use AI for outlines and first drafts only, then rewrite heavily
- Add specific examples from your life, not generic ones
- Include opinions. AI won't.

### Clickbait That Tanks Trust

**The pattern**: Headline promises X, content delivers Y.

"This One Trick Doctors Don't Want You To Know (Spoiler: It's Sleep)"

This works once. Then your readers feel betrayed and leave forever.

**Better approach**: Make promises you keep. A headline about sleep with content about sleep.

### Inconsistent Voice (The Confusing Brand)

You sound like a CEO on LinkedIn, a guru on Twitter, and a friend in your newsletter. Readers leave because they don't know what to expect.

**Fix**: Choose one voice. Use it everywhere (with tone adjustments for context).

### Publishing Without Strategy (The Scattered Approach)

Writing random blog posts without a plan:
- No pillar structure
- No topic clusters
- No internal linking
- No editorial calendar
- No measurement

**Result**: 100 unrelated posts that drive no SEO authority or reader loyalty.

**Fix**: Define pillars first. Plan 90 days of content. Then write.

### Ignoring Distribution

You wrote the best blog post ever. Nobody reads it because you didn't share it.

**Distribution checklist:**
- Email list: Send it (4 times in different ways)
- Social media: Share on all platforms with different angles
- Paid amplification: $100-500 budget to boost reach
- Community: Share in relevant forums, Slack groups, Reddit (not spammy)
- Influencers: Ask people to share it

Great writing without distribution is journaling. Not publishing.

### Writing Inconsistently

Inconsistency kills growth. You can't build authority with 1 post a month.

**Minimum to be "active":**
- 2-4 blog posts per month
- 3-5 LinkedIn posts per week
- 5-10 Twitter posts per week
- 1 newsletter per week

Pick ONE channel to be excellent at. Then add others.

## 11. Getting Started: Your First 30 Days

1. **Week 1**: Define your voice. Write the voice guidelines.
2. **Week 2**: Create your content pillars. Outline 4 blog posts.
3. **Week 3**: Write those 4 posts. Publish one. Schedule the others.
4. **Week 4**: Start an email list. Send your first newsletter. Share on social.

That's it. Month 2, you increase volume. Month 3, you systemize.

## Recap: The Flux Operating System

- **Find your voice**: Excavate it from your writing, don't invent it
- **Systemize writing**: Batch, template, swipe files
- **Build pillars**: Define what you're known for
- **Publish consistently**: Regular rhythm beats sporadic brilliance
- **Repurpose ruthlessly**: One piece, many formats
- **Measure what matters**: Views, engagement, conversions, authority
- **Avoid slop**: AI, clickbait, inconsistency, randomness
- **Distribute everything**: Writing without distribution is invisible

Content is the long game. Flux is your playbook.
