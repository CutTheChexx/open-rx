# OPEN RX: BUILD

## The Operational Chaos Prescription for Trades and Project-Based Businesses

You're not running a job—you're running a business. **Build** is the compound skill that treats the chaos of estimates, job costing, scheduling, and crew management. Whether you're a general contractor, electrician, plumber, painter, landscaper, HVAC tech, or running any project-based service business, the principles here apply. This isn't management theory. It's what separates profitable shops from busy ones.

---

## ESTIMATING: THE FIRST CRITICAL OPERATION

An estimate is your binding promise to a price. Get it wrong and you eat the loss.

### Cost Estimation Framework

**Three-layer approach:**

1. **Material cost** — What you actually pay suppliers (not list price)
2. **Labor cost** — Hours × your fully loaded labor rate
3. **Overhead + markup** — Don't confuse margin with markup

**Material takeoff (TO):**
- Don't estimate by gut. Measure or count. Print plans, use scale ruler, count fixtures/outlets/linear feet.
- Request detailed specs from architects. "Ceramic tile" is not a spec—"6×6 subway tile, matte glaze" is.
- Check supplier pricing before estimating. Prices move weekly.
- Add waste factor by trade:
  - Drywall/framing: 10%
  - Tile: 15% (cuts, breakage)
  - Electrical: 5% (wire, boxes)
  - Plumbing: 8% (fittings, adaptation)
  - Paint: 5%
  - Landscaping: 12% (soil compaction, plant loss)

**Labor calculation:**
- Know your crew's production rates. Not industry averages—yours. Track it.
- Fully loaded labor rate = (hourly wage + payroll taxes + benefits + insurance) ÷ billable hours per year
  - Example: $35/hr wage + 40% burden = $49/hr fully loaded
- Time estimates by task, not by job. "Electrical rough-in" has different rates than "trim-out"
- Add 15% for site logistics (travel, setup, cleanup, rework)
- Never use your best crew's speed. Use your average crew's speed.

**Markup vs. margin—they're not the same:**
- Markup: markup = (selling price - cost) ÷ cost
  - 50% markup on $1000 cost = $1500 selling price
- Margin: margin = (selling price - cost) ÷ selling price
  - 50% margin on $1000 cost = $2000 selling price
- Confusion kills shops. Use margin for financial planning. Use markup for pricing.

### Trade-Specific Estimate Templates

**General Contractor:**
- Site prep/demolition
- Foundation/structural
- Framing (labor-intensive, high waste)
- MEP rough-ins (coordinate with subs)
- Insulation/drywall
- Finishes (flooring, trim, paint)
- Final systems (plumbing, electrical, HVAC commissioning)
- Contingency: 10% of base cost (document what it covers)

**Electrician:**
- Materials: wire, boxes, breakers, fixtures, equipment
- Labor: rough-in, trim-out, testing, commissioning (rates differ by phase)
- Inspections/permits
- Emergency calls (charge 2–3× standard rate)
- Service upgrades (entirely different animal—beware hidden issues)

**Plumber:**
- Materials: pipe, fittings, fixtures, traps, vents
- Labor: rough-in, trim, pressure test, final connections
- Permits/inspections
- Standby rates (call-backs, emergency service)
- Specialty work: soldering, welding (charge premium)

**Painter:**
- Surface prep (don't underestimate—80% of quality)
- Paint cost (don't cheap out, premium paint covers better)
- Labor: primer, finish coats, trim, cleanup
- Drywall repair/spackle (often miscalculated)
- Protection/masking (time killer)

**Landscaper:**
- Material: soil, mulch, plants, hardscape, amendments
- Labor: excavation, grading, planting, irrigation, hardscape install
- Equipment rental
- Site cleanup and debris removal
- Seasonal adjustments (winter pricing, freeze risk)

**HVAC:**
- Equipment: furnace, AC, ductwork, thermostat
- Labor: removal, installation, testing, balancing, commissioning
- Permits/inspections
- Emergency service (nights/weekends = 1.5–2× rate)
- Extended warranty (revenue stream, price it)

## JOB COSTING: KNOW YOUR ACTUAL PERFORMANCE

You bid based on estimates. Reality happens. Track it.

### Actual vs. Estimated Tracking

**Weekly cost capture:**
- Labor: time cards by job, phase, and trade
- Materials: actual purchases tied to job (not "general" purchases)
- Equipment: rental hours, tool wear allocation
- Subcontractors: invoiced amounts, change orders
- Compare to estimate weekly, not at project close

**Cost codes structure:**
- Major category (Sitework, Framing, MEP, Finishes, etc.)
- Subcategory (Rough-in, Trim-out, Repair, etc.)
- Task (Install 2×4 studs, Run 1" conduit, etc.)
- Track hours and $ by code

**Red flags—investigate immediately:**
- Labor 20%+ over estimate on any phase
- Material overage without documented change order
- Subcontractor overruns without scope change
- Equipment sitting idle on-site (rent ends, remove it)

### Change Order Management

**The change order is your legal protection and your paper trail.**

- Never start work on a scope change verbally. Ever.
- Change order form includes:
  - Original scope line item (reference estimate line)
  - New scope description (specific, measurable)
  - Reason (customer-initiated, unforeseen condition, design change, owner request)
  - Cost impact (labor + materials + overhead %)
  - Schedule impact (days added/subtracted)
  - Customer signature and date
  - Approval before work starts

**Unforeseen conditions (the buried foundation clause):**
- Document the condition with photos
- Stop work until documented
- Estimate correction scope (often 2–3× initial thought)
- Present to client immediately; don't "eat it"
- Track and price by trade severity

### Profit/Loss per Job

**Monthly P&L review by job:**
- Revenue: contract amount + approved change orders
- Cost of goods: materials, subs, equipment
- Labor: crew wages + burdens
- Overhead allocated: (monthly overhead ÷ billable hours) × job hours
- Gross profit %: (revenue - COGS - labor) ÷ revenue
- Target: 15–25% (depends on trade, risk, market)

**Jobs running underwater?**
- Cut scope immediately—re-estimate remaining work
- Reassign crew (if one is inefficient, swap them out)
- Stop scope creep—enforce change orders
- Don't compound the loss by staying on job too long

### Overhead Allocation

**Three methods:**

1. **Per hour:** $50K annual overhead ÷ 1000 billable hours = $50/hr allocation
2. **Percentage of revenue:** 18% of project revenue (common rule)
3. **Per dollar of labor:** $100K overhead ÷ $500K labor = 20% labor multiplier

- Use one method consistently
- Allocate overhead to every job, not "only big ones"
- Overhead creep kills profit—track it monthly

## SCHEDULING: TIME IS YOUR INVENTORY

You can't manufacture more of it. Waste it and it's gone.

### Project Timelines and Critical Path Basics

**Build your schedule in phases:**

1. Mobilization: permits, inspections, site setup (1–2 weeks)
2. Rough-in: structural, MEP, framing (varies by project)
3. Inspection points: building dept checkpoints, client walkthroughs
4. Installation: finishes, fixtures, systems (longest phase usually)
5. Final: punch list, closeout, client sign-off (2–3 weeks)

**Critical path (simple version for small shops):**
- Activities that delay completion if delayed themselves
- Example: Foundation pours are critical; landscaping is not
- Compress critical path first, not other work
- Build 10–15% schedule buffer into overall timeline

**Logistics sequence matters:**
- Rough-ins before drywall
- Inspections before covering walls
- Paint before flooring (except bathroom tile)
- Final cleanup before handoff
- Get sequence wrong and you're cutting open walls ($$ fast)

### Resource Allocation

**Don't overcommit crews:**
- If you have 8 electricians and 3 jobs, don't promise 3 jobs starting same day
- Assign crew leads 2 weeks ahead minimum
- Pre-stage materials 1 week before crew arrival
- Have a bench (one crew doing admin work, follow-ups, bids)

**Subcontractor coordination:**
- No "call you when ready" agreements—lock dates
- Build 2-day buffer between major sub switches
- Inspect sub work before they leave site
- Pay subs on time; they'll prioritize your jobs

### Managing Weather Delays (Outdoor Trades)

- Factor weather into timeline: 10% contingency days/week in winter regions
- Plan indoor work for bad weather windows
- Have crew ready for makeup schedule (nights/Saturdays if needed)
- Communicate delay cause to client immediately (protects schedule claim)
- Track weather delays separately—affects project profitability

### Gantt Alternatives for Small Shops

You don't need software. You need visibility.

- **Whiteboard wall:** Week-by-week, crews by row, jobs by column
- **Weekly crew huddle:** 15 min Friday, next week's assignments
- **Color coding:** Red (critical), yellow (at risk), green (on track)
- **Monthly calendar:** Major milestones to client (shows progress)

## PROJECT MANAGEMENT: THE DAILY GRIND

### Job Phases and Milestone Tracking

**Five accountability checkpoints per project:**

1. **Kickoff:** Site walk, crew briefing, safety review, schedule review (day 1–2)
2. **Rough-in inspection:** Building dept sign-off, owner satisfaction (mid-way)
3. **Mid-project review:** Cost vs. estimate check, schedule reality check (week 4–6)
4. **Final inspection:** Building dept, owner, all systems tested (near end)
5. **Closeout:** Punch list complete, warranties, referral ask (handoff)

### Punch Lists (The Hated But Critical Document)

**Punch list is not "cleanup"—it's incomplete scope.**

- Generate punch list at 80% completion, not 99%
- Client walks with you; you document (prevents surprises)
- Assign items to crew with deadline (not "sometime")
- Repaint, re-caulk, reinstall are punch list items—easy to defer
- Walk punch list twice: mid-list and pre-final
- Photo documentation for all punch items (protects against "you didn't fix it" claims)

### Daily Logs

**Crew lead fills out daily log—non-negotiable.**

Daily log includes:
- Date, job, crew names
- Hours worked (per person)
- Work completed (phase, scope, % of task)
- Materials used/received
- Subs on site
- Issues encountered (delays, defects, rework)
- Safety incidents (even near-misses)
- Weather conditions
- Next day plan

**Use:** Matches cost codes to hours, documents scope completion, protects you in disputes.

### Photo Documentation

- Photo milestones: Before, rough-in, final (minimum)
- Problem photos: Defects, unforeseen conditions, change order scope
- Phase-by-phase: Ties to punch list, shows progress to owner
- Geotag when possible (timeline proof)
- Store organized: Job number/date/phase naming

### Client Communication Cadence

**Weekly is standard, not over-communicating:**

- **Monday:** Week plan (what's happening, what you need from client)
- **Wednesday:** Mid-week check-in (any issues, timeline on track?)
- **Friday:** Week recap (progress, next week's plan, requests)
- **Monthly:** Formal invoice + progress photos + financial update
- **Issue-triggered:** Problem detected → client notified same day

**Pro tip:** Always call before emailing bad news. Email documents the conversation.

## INVOICING & PAYMENTS: YOUR CASH FLOW

### Progress Billing (Not Percent Complete)

**Bill monthly, tied to work completed + material delivered:**

- Invoice line items match estimate line items
- Description: what you completed this month
- Percentage of line item (not percentage of job)
- Example: "Framing—wall studs (70% complete): $14,000"

**Retainage (Holdback):**
- Typical: 5–10% held until project completion
- Define "completion": punch list done, inspections passed, warrantied
- Release retainage within 30 days of completion
- Retainage is not your working capital—budget without it

### Payment Terms Strategy

- **Lump-sum contracts:** 50% upfront, 50% at completion (protects both sides)
- **Cost-plus contracts:** 50% upfront, rest monthly with retainage
- **Time & materials:** 50% upfront, invoiced weekly, net-30
- **Deposits:** Require for any job over $5K (covers materials exposure)

**Collections baseline:**
- Invoice due net-30 (not "net-whenever")
- Charge 1.5% monthly interest on past-due (18% APY)
- Stop work if 60 days past due
- Lien rights vary by state—know your state law

### Lien Waivers (Mechanic's Lien Protection)

**You work, you're owed a lien if unpaid.**

- Get signed lien waiver before final payment (subs too)
- Conditional waiver: "Upon payment" (protects you)
- Unconditional waiver: Only after check clears (safest)
- Your state has statutory forms—use them
- No lien waiver = no final payment (until signed)

## CREW MANAGEMENT: YOUR BIGGEST ASSET

### Hiring and Onboarding

**Hire slow, fire fast.**

**What to look for:**
- Reliability (track record > interview charm)
- Craftsmanship (portfolio, references from past jobs)
- Safety mindset (no "safety is optional" people)
- Coachability (willing to learn your systems)

**Background checks and certifications:**
- Drive record (if driving company vehicle)
- Criminal history (job-site safety/client homes)
- License verification (electrician, plumber, GC)
- Insurance (if independent contractor)

**Onboarding:**
- Safety orientation (day 1, mandatory)
- Your processes (daily log, change order procedure, communication)
- Tools and PPE assignment
- Expectations (quality, timeliness, communication)
- 90-day probation is reasonable

### Time Tracking and Productivity Metrics

**Track hours by job, not just "worked."**

- Daily crew log or time clock (eliminate guessing)
- Billable vs. unbillable hours separated
- Productivity rate: billable hours ÷ total hours worked
- Target: 75–80% billable (15–20% admin, training, travel)
- Low performers (60% billable) get retrained or replaced

**Crew productivity metrics:**
- Production rate per task (square feet, linear feet, units per hour)
- Quality defect rate (rework = crew inefficiency)
- Safety incidents (leading indicator of problems)
- Customer satisfaction feedback per crew

### Safety Basics

**You're liable. Make it non-negotiable.**

- Daily toolbox talks (5 min, one topic per day)
- PPE requirement (hard hat, vest, glasses, boots)
- OSHA basics training for all (online, inexpensive)
- Incident reporting (document everything)
- Workers comp insurance (non-negotiable)
- Safety violations = suspension, second violation = termination

### Training Systems

**Build internal knowledge base:**

- SOPs for common tasks (how you install that fixture, your standard)
- Photo guides (show correct installation, not just "install this")
- Mentorship: experienced crew with new crew on same task (1 week overlap)
- Quarterly workshops: new product training, technique updates
- Certification reimbursement (EPA RoHS, CPR, etc.)

## CLIENT MANAGEMENT: THE RELATIONSHIP IS THE BUSINESS

### Scope Creep Prevention

**Every request is a potential scope change.**

"While you're here, can you..." = Change order.

**Triage process:**
1. Client makes request
2. "That's outside the contract scope. Let me price that change."
3. You estimate
4. Change order for approval + signature
5. Work doesn't start until signed

**No verbal agreements. Period.**

### Change Order Process

**You already have a template. Use it consistently.**

- Existing scope: what the contract covers
- New scope: specific, measurable
- Cost: material + labor + 20% overhead
- Timeline impact: does it delay the project?
- Get signature before work starts

**Why this matters:** A verbal "I'll do it for $500" becomes "You promised" when the client's memory differs.

### Expectation Setting (Before Contract)

**The walkthrough:**

- Show customer how you'll sequence work
- Explain how change orders work (important distinction)
- Clarify what's included (paint colors, fixture selections, etc.)
- Explain inspection requirements (timeline delays)
- Discuss payment schedule
- Define "done" (what punch list items are, what aren't)

### Warranty Handling

**You're warrantying your work, not products.**

- Your labor: 1 year (industry standard)
- Products: manufacturer warranty (pass through to customer)
- Document warranty: what you fixed, when, if any follow-up work needed
- Warranty claim response time: 48 hours (call, assess, estimate fix)
- Warranty work: bill it if it's customer damage, don't if it's your defect

**Keep 2% reserve per job for warranty claims (usually not needed, but budget it).**

### Getting Referrals (Your Best Marketing)

**Referrals come from satisfied clients, not asking.**

- Do the job right (exceeds expectations, not just completes)
- Leave site cleaner than you found it
- Follow-up call 1 week post-completion ("How's everything working?")
- Send thank-you note (physical card, handwritten)
- At 6 months: "Check-in, anything need attention?"
- At 12 months: "One-year follow-up, everything still good?"

**Then ask:** "I appreciate your business. Would you be comfortable referring me to friends needing similar work?"

## TOOL & MATERIAL MANAGEMENT

### Inventory Basics

**You have money sitting in your yard and truck.**

- Monthly inventory count (tools, consumables, materials for active jobs)
- High-theft items flagged (power tools, copper wire)
- Consumables reorder point (when to order before stockout)
- Vendor lead times (you need to know; affects scheduling)

### Supplier Relationships

- **Primary supplier:** Best prices, payment terms negotiated
- **Secondary supplier:** Backup for emergency needs
- **Volume discounts:** Calculate breakeven (bulk vs. carrying cost)
- **Payment terms:** Net-30 standard; negotiate to net-45 with volume
- **Returns policy:** Clarify upfront (unused vs. defective)

### Bulk Buying Strategy

**Buy in bulk for recurring materials, not "might need."**

- Lumber, drywall, fasteners: quarterly bulk buy
- Paint: negotiate annual contract with supplier
- Plumbing: negotiate fixture pricing upfront for the year
- Electrical: establish distributor account for crew pricing

**The trap:** Buying bulk because price is good = waste if you're sitting on it 6 months.

### Tool Tracking

**Tools are assets; track them.**

- Tool inventory by crew (assign responsibility)
- Tool life: depreciate over 3–5 years
- Maintenance: power tools need regular service
- Lost/damaged: crew pays 50% (creates accountability)
- Tool budget: 2–3% of annual revenue

## GROWTH OPERATIONS: SCALING WITHOUT BREAKING

### When to Hire (The Expansion Question)

**Don't hire to fill work—hire when you can't cover work.**

**Metrics for adding headcount:**

1. You have bids going unfilled (you're turning away revenue)
2. You're running crew 6 days/week consistently (burnout incoming)
3. Overtime is >10% of payroll (unsustainable)
4. You have 3+ months of pipeline visibility (job security for hire)

**Hiring sequence:**
- First hire: trusted foreman/crew lead (they manage, you bid/admin)
- Second hire: crew member (now you have a core team)
- Third hire: admin/office (one person can't estimate, invoice, and schedule)

### Fleet Management Basics

**Company vehicles are expensive.**

- Lease vs. buy: lease for trucks (maintenance included), buy tools vans if long-term
- Maintenance schedule: every 5K miles, oil changes, fluid checks
- Fuel tracking: separate fuel card, tied to vehicle (catches waste)
- Insurance: commercial auto required (personal doesn't cover business use)
- GPS/telematics: expensive but shows crew efficiency and safety

**Tool van is rolling inventory—organize it.**

### Insurance Requirements (Non-Negotiable)

**You're liable. Protect yourself.**

- General liability: $1M–$2M coverage (standard)
- Workers compensation: required by law (most states)
- Commercial auto: if you own vehicles
- Tools/equipment floater: if you carry expensive tools
- Completed operations: covers claims after job done
- Umbrella: $1M–$5M over base policies

**Cost:** 8–12% of payroll annually. Budget it.

### Licensing and Bonding

**Varies by state, trade, and contract type.**

**General contractor license:**
- Most states require it for contracts over $X (varies $1K–$20K)
- Requires experience, exam, maybe bonding
- Renewal annually

**Trade licenses:**
- Electrician, plumber, HVAC: license + continuing education
- Verify your crew's licenses quarterly

**Bonding:**
- Bid bond: guarantees you'll enter contract (requested on large bids)
- Performance bond: guarantees you'll complete work (required on public jobs)
- Payment bond: guarantees you'll pay subs and suppliers
- Cost: 1–2% of contract value (budget into bid)

## ANTI-PATTERNS: WHAT KILLS SHOPS

### Underbidding to Win Work

**The death spiral:**
- You bid low to win
- You make no profit
- You cut corners to survive
- Quality suffers
- Customer complains
- You fix it (eating more cost)
- You go out of business

**The fix:** Bid your rate. Let competitors underbid. You'll be in business next year.

### No Change Order Process

**Verbal agreements = disputes.**

- Customer: "I thought that was included."
- You: "That's a change. It costs $X."
- Customer: "You should have told me."
- Dispute wins for customer (she's less motivated to remember wrong).

**The fix:** Change order every change. Document it. Both sign.

### Ignoring Overhead in Estimates

**You bid labor + materials, but you have a building, truck, insurance, payroll admin.**

- If overhead is 30% and you don't allocate it, every job loses 30%.
- You're financing the business with your own pocket.

**The fix:** Allocate overhead to every job. Every one. No exception.

### Growing Too Fast Without Systems

**You go from 3 crews to 8 crews.**

- Foreman can't supervise 8 crews alone (chaos)
- No daily logs = no cost tracking (no idea if you're profitable)
- Crew doesn't know your standards (quality drops)
- You lose money faster and bigger

**The fix:** Hire people to manage before you hire crew. Systems first, growth second.

### Verbal Agreements

**"I'll pay you $500 for this extra work." Or "The owner said it's fine."**

- No signature = he said/she said
- When dispute arises, you lose (burden of proof is on you)

**The fix:** Email confirmation at minimum. Change order signature is gold.

### Not Tracking Actual Costs

**You estimate, bid, do the work, invoice. Then you wonder why you made $2K on a $100K job.**

- No visibility into where money went
- Can't improve next estimate (don't know what went wrong)
- Crew inefficiency invisible
- Material waste invisible

**The fix:** Weekly cost capture. Match actual to estimate. Review monthly.

## THE OPERATIONAL EDGE

**Estimating**, **job costing**, **scheduling**, **project management**, **invoicing**, **crew management**—these are where the profit lives or dies. Not in the craft. Not in the sales pitch. In the details of how you run the operation.

Your margin isn't set at the bid. It's set at the daily log, the change order process, the crew lead who knows your standard, the invoice that covers what you did, the crew retention that lets you stay out of constant hiring mode.

**Run the operation tight, and the rest follows.**
